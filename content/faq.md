# FAQ

<details open>
    <summary><strong>What was your first programming language and computer?</strong></summary>
    <p>My first language was BASIC on an <a href="https://ro.wikipedia.org/wiki/HC_91" target="_blank">HC91</a>, a Romanian microcomputer based on the Z80. My parents bought me one in 93.</p>
</details>

<details open>
    <summary><strong>What was the first program you ever wrote?</strong></summary>
    <p>The first thing I ever "coded" was a joint effort with my dad, who was actually trying to learn how to program at that time. We sat down together and managed to draw a bicycle on that <a href="https://ro.wikipedia.org/wiki/HC_91" target="_blank">HC91</a>. A total breakthrough.</p>
</details>

<details open>
    <summary><strong>Low-level control or High-level abstraction?</strong></summary>
    <p>Low-level control and a good deal of abstraction on top. My favorite programming language by far is C, mostly because it allows me to be in full (low-level) control of what I am building. Of course, this comes with caveats. It was very hard for me to switch to Java in my career, but funnily enough, Java is now my second favorite language.</p>
</details>

<details open>
    <summary><strong>Static typing or Dynamic typing?</strong></summary>
    <p>Static typing. I simply don't see any reason to use dynamic typing unless I am building something very ethereal, a DSL, or a simple script. Other than that, the mere thought of using dynamic typing makes me uncomfortable.</p>
</details>

<details open>
    <summary><strong>Monolith or Microservices?</strong></summary>
    <p>A well-written monolith. I prefer that to spaghetti services. Microservices gone wrong often become a nightmare where services are sending information into the void of space, hoping an event-driven alien might be listening. This is far worse than having a reliable, old-school monolith where you actually know what is happening and where. In my experience, the vast majority of projects I've worked on, with very few exceptions, simply never needed the complexity of microservices to begin with.</p>
</details>

<details open>
    <summary><strong>CLI or GUI?</strong></summary>
    <p>Both. With age, I’ve come to prefer minimalistic GUIs. I also never quite understood the fuss about Vim or Emacs; for me, the speed of writing and editing code has always been significantly greater than the speed of debugging it. Consequently, a feature-rich debugger is far more important to my workflow than a terminal-based editor. I might be ignorant on the subject, for which I apologize, but a good GUI (with lots of shortcuts, of course) just makes more sense for the way I work.</p>
</details>

<details open>
    <summary><strong>Optimize later or Optimize early?</strong></summary>
    <p>Optimize whenever you can.</p>
</details>

<details open>
    <summary><strong>Test-first or Build-first?</strong></summary>
    <p>Build first. TDD hasn't convinced me yet. While the theory is noble, many people (including myself) dislike it because it often leads to a "test-induced design" where the architecture is forced to accommodate the tests rather than the actual problem. It happens, and I've seen it happen far more often than I should have.</p>
</details>

<details open>
    <summary><strong>Explicit configuration or Convention-based?</strong></summary>
    <p>Explicit configuration if possible.</p>
</details>

    <p>I have a soft spot for music, specifically "forgotten" progressive rock albums from the '70s and '80s, alongside contemporary indie acts. </details>
