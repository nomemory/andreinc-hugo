Some jokes write themselves. In our case, the electrician wrote it.

Let me give you a little context. 

Summers in Bucharest can get brutally hot, and when it gets that hot, your brain simply stops functioning. So on weekends, I prefer the cool breeze of the lakes surrounding our capital city. 

[Bucharest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bucharest) was actually built on a swampy area, so lakes are practically everywhere. One of the most amazing places to escape to just outside the city is the [Cernica Monastery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cernica_Monastery). Established in 1608, it sits peacefully on two islands right in the middle of Lake Cernica.

The view is absolutely stunning:

{{< img src="/images/2026-03-12-fresco-the-lightbringer/cernica.jpg" width="450" caption="Cernica Monastery on Lake Cernica" grayscale="false">}}

So, if you ever find yourself in Bucharest and want to avoid melting into the pavement, visit this retreat. It is breezy, the monks are incredibly nice, the gardens are beautiful, and you can actually use the quiet moments for some deep introspection.

And now for the funny part...

On the inside of the monastery wall there is a very old fresco featuring a devil painted in the classic Orthodox (Byzantine) style. Well, at some point the monastery went through some renovations, and someone had to update the electrical wiring. 

Because our hero the electrician wanted to preserve the surrounding frescoes, he made the executive decision to install the modern light switch directly into the devil's outstretched hand.

This is the picture I took, and I couldn't help myself to make it interactive, so please click on the light switch.

Why is this funny ? It's not that funny if I have to explain it...

The narrative begins in the Old Testament, specifically within the Book of Isaiah. The original Hebrew text contains a prophetic critique directed at a tyrannical Babylonian king. The prophet mocks the monarch's arrogance by comparing his fleeting empire to the morning star (actually the planet Venus) which shines brilliantly just before dawn but is quickly eclipsed by the sun. In old Hebrew language, this ruler is referred to as *Helel ben Shahar*, which translates to "shining one, son of the dawn." 

When the first scholars translated the Hebrew scriptures into Latin to create the [Vulgate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulgate), they rendered this metaphor using the standard Roman term for the morning star: Lucifer. The name is derived from the Latin words *lux* (meaning "light") and *ferre* (meaning "to bring"), the word was simply a literal translation describing a planet or a star. For a time it remained a an allegory for a human dictator. And it stayed like that for a while.

Years later, some early Christian theologians ([Origen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origen) and [Augustine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo)) reevalued this text. As the historical context of the Babylonian was forgotten, these scholars began to read the passage in Isaiah not merely as political poetry, but as a literal story.

To support this interpretation, they drew a theological parallel between the "fall" of the morning star in Isaiah and a specific verse in the  Gospel of Luke, where Jesus states, *"I saw Satan fall like lightning from heaven."*

By synthesizing these two distinct scriptural passages, early church scholars concluded that both verses described the exact same event. 

Consequently, the narrative of the babylonian king was put aside. "Lucifer" transitioned from a Latin noun describing Venus into the definitive proper name for the Devil before his rebellion, characterizing him as heaven's brightest and most beautiful angel prior to his fall from grace. 

Therefore, by placing the light switch directly into the hands of the painted demon, the electrician at the Cernica Monastery inadvertently created a... visual pun.
