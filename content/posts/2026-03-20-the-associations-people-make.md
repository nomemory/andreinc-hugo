Contrary to my GitHub username, **nomemory**, my ability to remember things is (still) quite good. It has always been the case, and for this reason I had it quite easy in school (up to a certain point). At the same time, I was lazier than average. *["You win some, lose some, it’s all the same"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3mbvWn1EY6g&t=15s)*.

In spite of that, I had trouble memorizing isolated terms, like lists of words, specific years and dates, the [Mendeleev table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mendeleev%27s_predicted_elements), or the nervous and muscular systems in anatomy. 

Like most people, sometimes without even realizing it, we apply the [Loci method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_loci) to remember things. We consciously and/or subconsciously associate a story, an image, a taste, or a scent with the things we want to remember. This association works both like a key in a hash table (when we want to access data) and as a trigger in a database, when a *[À la recherche du temps perdu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In_Search_of_Lost_Time)* madeleine moment happen to us. Some people are naturally better at this than others, to the point where they describe things via synesthesia. The Loci method and [synesthesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synesthesia) are actually related, as both rely on multisensory encoding. For reasons that are not fully understood, our brains prefer this. Synesthesia is a natural trait, while the Loci technique is a deliberate act. But the mechanisms work in similar ways.

Before I even read about it, my father saw me struggling with memorizing things, so he taught me to associate the silliest story with the thing I would forget. "A silly story is easier to remember," he said.

The first memory I have of this involves two mathematical terms that are not very popular in English, but we use them in our language ([Romanian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanian_language)): **Abscisă** ([Abscissa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abscissa_and_ordinate), another name for the x-axis of a coordinate system) and **Ordonată** ([Ordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abscissa_and_ordinate), the y-axis). For reasons beyond my understanding, my brain couldn't remember which was which. I mean, i could remind it for a day or two, but after of week, I was confusing them again, and again.

So my father made a joke. He said that I should imagine the **Ordonată** as a soldier sitting vertically, waiting to receive orders (*"ordine"*). And **Abscisă** as the same soldier having an [dental abscess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dental_abscess) (in our language *"absces la măsea / dinte"*), lying horizontally because of the pain inflicted by our body's response to bacteria.

This silly association stuck with me, and when I had trouble memorising things I was desperately searching for a silly association. 

The next one I still remember involves "calling the cops". So after understanding what the `sine` and `cosine` mean, I was making the confusion which is which. Somehow my brain decided `sine` is the first one and `cosine` is the second one, this one I could remember, but I was forgetting which cathete to use. In spoken Romanian the formulas for `sine` and `cosine` are:

<div class="mp mpc">
    \[
        \text{sinus} = \frac{\text{catetă opusă}}{\text{ipotenuză}} = \frac{\text{opposite side}}{\text{hypotenuse}}
    \]
    \[
        \text{cosinus} = \frac{\text{catetă alăturată}}{\text{ipotenuză}} = \frac{\text{adjacent side}}{\text{hypotenuse}}
    \]
</div>

So I found a letter combination **c**atetă **op**usă $\implies$ **cop** and **c**atetă **al**ăturată $\implies$ **call**.
