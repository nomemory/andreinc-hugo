Contrary to my GitHub username, nomemory, my memory is, no bragging, better than average. This was my blessing and my curse. It helped me glide through school with decent grades and minimal effort, but it also made me a bit lazier than I would've liked.

​If I read a poem a few times, I was able to recite it shortly after; or, if I were working on a math or physics problem, I could remember its solution months or even years later. The poem and the problem were telling a story, or they left some sort of visual footprint. Even the maps in Geography were telling stories to me.

​On the other hand, I had real trouble memorizing isolated terms, lists of words, specific years and dates, the Mendeleev table, or the nervous and muscular systems in anatomy. I had to invent small mental stories, simple mental images, or various weird associations just to remember them and avoid confusing the terms.

​For example, when I was learning about the Cartesian system, I knew there were two axes: Ox and Oy. In my native language, we used those terms, but also "Abscisă" and "Ordonată." I believe the terms are similar in English, though they are probably less common. Well, I had a hard time remembering which was which.

​Until I found the "saving logic." Thinking back, it's actually funny. For "Ordonată," I would imagine a soldier who was receiving orders and standing vertically at attention. For "Abscisă," I decided to think of a person who has an abscess (absces in Romanian) in their teeth and is lying in bed horizontally because they are in pain. Years later, I can still see that soldier every time I hear "Ordonată."

The I had the same problem when first encountering trigonometric functions. I knew there's sine and there's cosine. And I knew there are formulas involving both legs (cathete) and the hypotenuse in a right triangle, but in the beginning I always forgetting which was which. So I came up with the city name Upsala.
